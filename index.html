My Story

I grew up in the Middle East and the Sussex countryside. I knew nobody in the film business. I knew nobody in London. I had no plan.

At nineteen I moved to the city anyway and took a job temping at Barclays Bank. One evening I went to a party and got talking to a French cameraman. He was very handsome, very suave, had a beautiful girlfriend and the kind of easy confidence that comes from doing something you love for a living. He'd just finished a film about skateboarding. He was about to make one about bungee jumping. I had never met anyone like him.

I asked him how you got into that world. He said: go to Soho, find a job as a runner. Basically a tea boy. That was it. That was the advice.

The following week I wrote a CV, got on the Northern line and spent the afternoon pushing it through every door that looked remotely promising. A model agency. A record company. A photography studio. A casting agency. An architect's office. I had absolutely no idea what I was looking for. I just kept going.

One of those doors was 25 Noel Street. I walked up to the reception desk and before I could say a word, Camilla looked up and said my name. I had never seen her in my life. Turned out I'd gone to school with her brother. In a city of eight million people, where I knew practically nobody, she was sitting behind that particular desk on that particular afternoon.

She took me upstairs and introduced me to Sharon. Sharon was in charge of hiring the runners at the Moving Picture Company. I got the job. I handed in my notice at Barclays and started the following Monday.

I think about that afternoon a lot. A different door. A different day. Jean-Pierre goes to a different party. Camilla sits at a different desk. Sharon says no. I go back to Barclays.

Thirty-five years of film. Gone, just like that.

That was 1990. I'm still here — and I'm so very grateful.

Tea boy. Runner. 2nd AD. First AD. Producer.

Ford Explorer: Lexie Limitless World Record Finale

Live Experiential — Ford

We managed a rolling closure of twenty miles of the South of France coast road into Nice. As the convoy moved through each section, the route was sealed ahead and released behind — keeping the road clear without shutting it down entirely. A convoy of twenty historic Ford vehicles. Motorcycle cameramen whose day job is filming the Tour de France. Helicopters, drones, and multiple fixed cameras the length of the route.

The entire event was broadcast live online. To maintain a continuous live feed across twenty miles, we put a relay aircraft at 3,000 feet — signal up from the helicopter, across to the plane, down to the OB truck.

Lexie arrived into Nice where Ford CEO Jim Farley joined her for the final mile to the finish line.

Everest

Live Broadcast / Television — On-Mountain Producer

The British Army mounted a 2006 attempt on the West Ridge of Everest — a route no British team had ever climbed. What that understates is just how rarely anyone has climbed it. Fewer than a dozen climbers have ever summited via the West Ridge and Hornbein Couloir. More people have died attempting it than have reached the top.

Twenty-two soldiers. Three years of preparation. They turned back 800 metres short of the summit. In that context, how far they got matters.

The expedition was filmed for television and served as a national Army recruitment campaign. I was the on-mountain producer at 6,000 metres for twelve weeks, responsible for all filming and live satellite broadcasts from the mountain.

It was a pivotal project. Twelve weeks at altitude, cut off from the world, working with people who had committed everything to a single goal. The friendships it forged have lasted twenty years.

Imagination were given a brief. Take a desert island in the Red Sea and turn it into a Glastonbury weekend for billionaires. Sindalla Island. Saudi Arabia. The largest gathering of superyachts in history.

A desert island. No infrastructure. No facilities. Culminating in everything you could ever wish for, including a Michelin star restaurant.

To make it work, you need to populate an island. That meant flying from the UK the full human spectrum of festival talent. It was like a real life Mr Ben. Jugglers. Chefs. Dancers. DJs. Singers. Circus performers. Kite surfers. Fire breathers. Yoga instructors. Personal trainers. Face painters. Catering staff. Massage therapists.

Two films. The Island is the finished event. From Nothing is how it came to exist.

The Early Years

|

This is the nineties and the early noughties. This is where it started. First Assistant Director on music videos, commercials, children's drama, boy bands, girl bands, the full glorious nonsense of the pop era. Rover Cars before they went bust. More videos than I care to remember — and some of the biggest songs of the decade came with the cheesiest videos.

I know, because I made a lot of them.

Diana Ross — Not Over You Yet

First Assistant Director

Shot in the Tate & Lyle building in East London. We wrapped around midnight.
