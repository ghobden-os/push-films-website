Second Act.

Four new directions. More cinematic. Referencing film titles, lens optics, and the visual grammar of the screen. Cormorant Garamond for any set type — a proper film serif.

A
Widescreen

Two thin gold bars close in from the edges — the letterbox of cinema. The mark lives inside a 2.39:1 frame. GH appears as the bars lock into position. Restrained, authoritative. The format itself does the talking.

Reference: Fincher titles. Nolan main-on-ends. 2001.

Screen
Print

Notes

  • The gold lines slide in from left to right — the frame arrives before the content.
  • Bars act as letterboxing — referencing widescreen cinema, not a design choice but a format.
  • On print: the bars read as natural margins. The whole thing fits a business card perfectly.
  • Name in top bar / discipline in bottom — like a film's opening and closing credits positions.
B
Iris

A circle draws itself — the lens ring. Then the aperture opens, revealing the initials inside. Camera. Projection. Cinema as optics. The iris is the first thing a frame sees.

Reference: Kubrick's eye. Saul Bass. The Criterion Collection.

Screen
Print

Notes

  • The lens ring draws first (0.8s). Tick marks appear. Then the iris opens and GH draws inside.
  • In print: the circle reads as a clean seal or badge. Strong on embossed letterhead.
  • Works well at all sizes — the ring provides a clear boundary at small scale.
  • Could animate on hover on the website (subtle re-draw on mouse enter).
C
Title Card

No mark. No monogram. Just the name — set in Cormorant Garamond, stacked, spaced perfectly. Each line arrives from below. A rule draws between them. This is how films begin. The name is the mark.

Reference: A24. Mubi. The Criterion spine. Powell & Pressburger.

Screen
Print

Notes

  • Cormorant Garamond 300 weight — one of the most cinematic typefaces available. Used widely in arthouse film.
  • No mark needed at this size — the letterforms carry the identity entirely.
  • "GREG" and "HOBDEN" arrive independently, with the rule drawing between them.
  • On print: this is a letterhead. Set at 72pt on A4, nothing else on the page.
  • The GH monogram from concepts 1–3 can pair with this as a compact variant.
D
Hairline

The same GH geometry but at hairline weight — the thinnest possible stroke. Precision over personality. The mark feels like it was drawn with a single hair. Confident enough to need almost nothing.

Reference: Bottega Veneta. Jil Sander. Early Helmut Lang.

Screen
Print

Notes

  • Stroke-width 0.75px — at the very limit of what a printer can reproduce. Print minimum: 0.5pt.
  • The hairline draws very slowly — 2.2s for the G alone. The slowness is intentional.
  • Pairs with Concept C (Title Card) — use this as the small mark, the full name as the wordmark.
  • On screen at small sizes (nav): renders cleanly on retina displays. May thin out on non-retina.
